Unit 9 - Data Structures

This unit contains the following lessons:

  • Lesson 1: Introduction to Data Structures
  • Lesson 2: Stacks
  • Lesson 3: Queues and Dequeues
  • Lesson 4: Linked Lists
  • Lesson 5: Trees and Binary Trees
  • Lesson 6: Hash Tables and Dictionaries (Review + Deep Dive)
  • Lesson 7: Choosing the Right Data Structure

Lesson 1 Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

  • explain what a data structure is and why different data structures matter.
  • understand the difference between built-in data structures (lists, dictionaries) and custom ones.
  • describe how choosing the right data structure affects program efficiency.
  • reflect on how God created the world with wonderful variety and order — each thing designed for its purpose — just as we choose different data structures for different tasks (Genesis 1:31; Ecclesiastes 3:1).

Lesson 2 Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

  • explain what a stack is and its core operations (push, pop, peek).
  • implement a stack using a Python list.
  • understand the Last-In-First-Out (LIFO) principle.
  • recognize real-world uses of stacks (undo feature, function call stack, browser history).
  • reflect on how stacks illustrate the biblical principle of “last shall be first” in God’s kingdom (Matthew 20:16).

Lesson 3 Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

  • explain what a queue is and its core operations (enqueue, dequeue).
  • implement a queue using a Python list or collections.deque.
  • understand the First-In-First-Out (FIFO) principle.
  • implement and use a dequeue (double-ended queue).
  • recognize real-world uses of queues (task scheduling, print queues, customer service lines).
  • reflect on how queues reflect fairness and order, similar to how God calls us to wait patiently and serve one another (James 5:7-8; Philippians 2:3-4).

Lesson 4 Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

  • explain what a linked list is and how it differs from a Python list.
  • implement a simple singly linked list with nodes.
  • perform basic operations: insert, delete, and traverse.
  • understand the advantages and disadvantages of linked lists vs arrays/lists.
  • recognize that linked lists show how individual elements can be connected to form something greater, much like the body of Christ (1 Corinthians 12:12-27).

Lesson 5 Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

  • explain what a tree is and the concept of hierarchical data.
  • understand the structure of a binary tree and binary search tree.
  • perform basic tree traversals (in-order, pre-order, post-order).
  • recognize real-world uses of trees (file systems, decision trees, organizational charts).
  • reflect on how trees illustrate spiritual growth — rooted deeply, growing upward, and bearing fruit (Psalm 1:3; Colossians 2:6-7).

Lesson 6 Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

  • explain how hash tables work and why they provide fast lookups (average O(1)).
  • understand the relationship between Python dictionaries and hash tables.
  • discuss concepts of hashing, collisions, and collision resolution.
  • compare the efficiency of hash tables with lists and trees for different operations.
  • recognize that hash tables demonstrate how God can bring quick access and order even from complex or chaotic input.

Lesson 7 Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

  • compare the strengths and weaknesses of stacks, queues, deques, linked lists, trees, and hash tables.
  • choose the most appropriate data structure for a given problem or scenario.
  • implement small programs that effectively use different data structures.
  • analyze trade-offs between time complexity, space complexity, and simplicity.
  • reflect on how wisdom is shown in choosing the right tool for the right job — a principle that applies both in programming and in Christian living (Proverbs 4:7; Ecclesiastes 10:10).
